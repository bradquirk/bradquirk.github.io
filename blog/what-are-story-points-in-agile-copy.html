What are story points in agile?

15 June 2021 - 6 mins read time
Tags: Agile Scrum Ceremonies

What are story points in agile?

Story points are a unitless measure that helps agile teams to track the progress and complexity of their work. They are used to estimate the combined effort, duration and risk involved in completing a task, and they can be tracked over time to see how the team is managing their workload. This allows teams to adjust their plans and processes as needed in order to more effectively complete their work.

What is a user story?

User stories are a simple way for agile teams to capture the needs (and potential outcomes) of an end user as they relate to the product. Ideally, they are short enough in description to fit onto a post-it note, and are used to initiate conversations within the development team. User stories are short and discrete in nature, and can only have one particular state as it relates to a kanban board or delivery workflow (e.g. To Do, In Progress etc.). They should always be written from the reference point of the person that will use the implemented feature, rather than the perspective of the team implementing it. By doing this, each user story has a focus on value delivered, rather than the effort used to build something.

What are story points in agile?

Story points are a relative unit of measure that helps agile teams track the progress of their work. They are used to estimate the effort required to complete a task, and they can be tracked over time to see how the team is doing. This allows teams to adjust their plans and processes as needed in order to more effectively complete their work.

How do story points help agile teams track progress?

Story points help agile teams track progress by allowing them to estimate the effort required to complete a task. Although not referenced in the official Scrum Guide, a common practice for agile teams using story points is to track the total story points delivered as a ‘velocity’ metric.

The benefits of using velocity include:

  • Allowing agile teams to track the amount of complexity delivered over time

  • Help teams understand what a realistic commitment looks like at the start of each sprint

  • Help management understand how predictable the team’s delivery pipeline is, and where any potential continuous improvement opportunities lie

However, there are also limitations to using velocity:

  • Senior management often conflate velocity with team performance. This is often not the case, as story points are not a reflection of the value delivered

  • Velocity is easily gamed, which can lead to negative behaviours within the team. If a team is being told their velocity is now a performance target, they will inevitably start inflating their story point estimates to drive up velocity. This renders the metric meaningless

What are some best practices for using story points?

When using story points, it is important to keep the following in mind:

  • Use story points to estimate the combined effort, duration and complexity required to complete a task, not just the time it will take to complete the task.

  • Use story points to track the progress of the team as a whole, not the individual members of the team.

  • Keep the number of story points assigned to each task small. This will help the team to more accurately estimate the effort required to complete the task.

  • Use a consistent scale when assigning story points to user stories. This will help the team to compare the relative sizes of tasks.

Agile estimating and planning

Agile estimating and planning is an important part of using story points effectively. When estimating user stories, it is important to remember that story points are a unit of measure to track the progress of the team, not the individual members of the team.

Why Fibonacci for story points?

Fibonacci numbers are a sequence of numbers in which each number is the sum of the previous two. They are often used in business and finance because they are believed to be a predictor of trends. Fibonacci numbers can also be used to estimate the number of items in a set. For example, if a team has five items to complete, they could use the Fibonacci number sequence to estimate that it will take them eight story points to complete the user stories.

The typical Fibonacci scale used when assigning story points is as follows:

1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, 40, 100, ???

The Fibonacci sequence can be used to estimate the effort required to complete a task because it takes into account the fact that some user stories are more difficult than others. The sequence starts with a small number, such as 1, and then each subsequent number is the sum of the previous two. This means that the sequence gradually gets larger, which reflects the fact that some user stories will take longer to complete than others.

Using Fibonacci numbers to estimate story points can be helpful for agile teams because it allows them to more accurately reflect the difficulty of a task. It also helps teams to compare the relative sizes of user stories, which can be useful when planning their work.

T-shirt sizes in agile

An alternative to using a Fibonacci sequence is to instead use T-shirt sizing to assign relative estimates to user stories. For example:

S, M, L, XL, XXL, ???

When it comes to t shirt sizes in agile, similar to using story points, it’s important to keep in mind that they are not intended to be a precise measurement. Rather, they are used as a way to estimate the size of a task.

In closing

Story points are a unit of measure that helps agile teams track the progress of their work. They are used to estimate the effort required to complete a task, and they can be tracked over time to see how the team is doing. This information can help agile teams adjust their plans as needed to stay on track. There are a variety of methods that can be used to assign story points, like a Fibonacci sequence, and there are other alternatives such as T-shirt sizing.

When using story points to estimate the effort required for a task, it is important to keep in mind that they should be used to estimate the effort required for the team, not the individual members of the team. Finally, when assigning story points to user stories, it is best practice to use a consistent scale and to keep the number of story points small.
