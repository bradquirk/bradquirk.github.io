    The Value

    An important part of adopting an agile mindset is the acceptance that we operate in complex envrionments that are surrounded by uncertainty, both internally and externally. In order to have meaningful conversations about the degree of risk involved in a project or program of work we can use a Monte Carlo simulation, which involves simulating tens of thousands of potential outcomes based on historical performance, and summarising them as a distribution.

    After understanding the degree of risk involved, and an internal perception of what is 'acceptable', we can then seek to minimise the degree of uncertainty by increasing the predictability of teams' performance. More stability = less risk.

    Hypothesis

    I believe
    that building a Monte Carlo simulation to forecast the remaining work in a program
    will enable managers and leaders to understand and articulate the degree of risk and uncertainty that has been inherited
    and lead to structured conversations about optimising stability and predictability over speed and throughput
    I'll know I'm right when teams increase their predictability (e.g. by lowering WIP)
    and in turn produce a higher output and better outcomes for their customers.

    Method

    1. Collect up to ten days/weeks/sprints/months* worth of historical throughput
    2. Once the historical data has been input, the Monte Carlo simulator takes that throughput and models a Weibull Probability Distribution to ensure 'extreme' inputs don't skew the output
    3. The Monte Carlo sim then models 10,000 hypothetical outcomes by randomly selecting values from the Weibull curve
    4. Those 10,000 outcomes are then collected and aggregated into a cumulative distribution function, or CDF
    5. The CDF can then be used to articulate the '% Confidence' in

    * Note: The Simulation tool works regardless of the cadence or size of work items. Provided they are consistent you can simulate story points over sprints, releases over months etc.

    Insights

    Having run several dozen Monte Carlo simulations for large-scale programs of work in different environments, I have noted the following consistent behaviours:

    • We as people have a bias towards overestimating our own capability to deliver work. This is especially evidence in complex environments, where even experienced delivery practitioners are unable to predict what might happen in the short-medium term.
    • Once the simulation had completed and the managers/leaders were able to create a risk profile of their portfolio, they were able to have much more targeted, informed conversations with their teams about the challenges they face in uncertain environments. This usually lead to decisions based on compromise for both parties, negotiating what was truly feasible and high-value in an effort to discard waste and reduce uncertainty.
    • The teams that were focussed on predictability and consistency through WIP management usually had much lower risk profiles than those who instead optimised speed and output.

    Conclusion

    By creating a simulation tool that can create many 'what-if?' scenarios rapidly, teams and their leaders were much more aligned with regards to their priorities and workload throughout the course of delivery. This also enabled a risk-mitigation culture that in turn reduced the stress and pressure experienced by the teams, increasing their overall well-being and in the long term were higher-performing because of it.
